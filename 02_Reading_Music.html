Volume 2: Reading Music

Notation is a map of the keyboard. Learn to read it the way fluent readers actually do, by landmarks and intervals, not by memorizing 50 separate note names.

Why reading is non negotiable. Falling note apps and YouTube hand tutorials can teach you one song at a time, forever. Reading teaches you every song at once. It is also how you will study chord voicings, analyze harmony for production, and learn repertoire no one has made a tutorial for. Ten minutes a day for a few months buys you fluency for life. The method here is the one used by the Royal Conservatory tradition and reading specialists: landmarks first, intervals second, note names as confirmation.

1. The staff is a picture of the keyboard

Music sits on a staff of 5 lines and 4 spaces. Higher on the staff means higher on the keyboard, to your right. Each line or space is one white key step. That is the entire trick: notation is not a code, it is a drawing of up and down.

Piano music uses two staffs joined together, the grand staff. The top staff carries a treble clef (the swirl) and is usually played by the right hand. The bottom carries a bass clef (the two dots) for the left hand. Between the two staffs, floating on its own short line, lives middle C, the very key your right thumb knows from C position.

Middle C (C4) written on the treble staff (top) and the bass staff (bottom). Same key on the piano, one small ledger line below the treble staff or above the bass staff. The two staffs meet in the middle at this note.

2. Landmarks: the three notes that anchor everything

Fluent readers do not name every note. They know a few anchor notes instantly and read everything else as a distance from an anchor. Your three core landmarks:

Treble G (G4) sits on the line the treble clef curls around, second line up. Bass F (F3) sits on the line between the two dots of the bass clef, second line down. Middle C sits between the staffs. The clefs literally point at their landmarks, which is why they are called the G clef and F clef.
The same three landmarks on the keyboard. F3 is below middle C, G4 above.

Drill (daily, week 2 onward): on the Tools page flashcards, drill just these three until recognition is instant. Then add the secondary landmarks below. Instant means under one second, no counting lines.

Secondary landmarks, added over weeks 3 to 6: Treble C (C5, third space of the treble staff), Bass C (C3, second space of the bass staff), the bottom line of the bass staff (G2) and the top line of the treble staff (F5). With seven anchors spread across the grand staff, no note is ever more than a step or two from something you know cold.

3. Reading by interval: repeats, steps, and skips

With landmarks in place, you read by answering one question per note: how far from the previous note, and which direction?

Repeat: same line or space as the last note. Same key, play it again.

Step (a 2nd): line to the very next space, or space to the very next line. On the piano: the neighboring white key. In your hand: the neighboring finger.

Skip (a 3rd): line to the next line, or space to the next space. Piano: skip one white key. Hand: skip one finger.

Repeats, steps, and skips from Treble G. Read the shape: same, up a step, up a step, down a skip, up a skip, down a step. Play it in C position without looking at your hand.

Larger intervals extend the same logic. A 4th spans four letter names (line to space, one gap between). A 5th spans five (line to line with a line skipped, or space to space with a space skipped), which is exactly thumb to pinky in a five finger position.

Intervals from middle C: 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th. Notice the visual logic, each interval is one notch wider on the staff. Volume 4 builds chords out of these same intervals.
Why intervals beat mnemonics. You may know "Every Good Boy Does Fine" for the treble lines (E G B D F) and "FACE" for the spaces, with "Good Boys Do Fine Always" and "All Cows Eat Grass" for bass lines (G B D F A) and spaces (A C E G). They are fine as a safety net and worth knowing. But counting up a mnemonic takes two seconds per note, reading by interval takes a fraction of one. Train intervals as the primary skill, keep mnemonics for double checking.

Ledger lines are the short lines that extend the staff, like middle C's. Notes can sit several ledger lines above or below any staff. Read them the same way: count steps from your nearest landmark.

5. Rhythm: how long notes last

A note's position says which key. Its shape says how long. Everything is measured in beats, the steady pulse you tap your foot to.

The note value family. Each shape is worth half the one before it: whole (4 beats), half (2), quarter (1), eighth (1/2). A dot after any note adds half its value again: dotted half = 3 beats, dotted quarter = 1.5.
Rests are counted silence, one for each note value: whole rest (hangs below the line, 4 beats), half rest (sits on the line, 2 beats), quarter rest (the squiggle, 1 beat). Rests are played, your hands release, the counting never stops.

Time signatures and bars

Music is divided into bars (measures) by vertical lines. The time signature at the start tells you how the bars are filled: the top number is beats per bar, the bottom number says which note gets one beat (4 means quarter note). So 4/4 means four quarter note beats per bar, 3/4 means three, the waltz feel. 4/4 is so common it is the default in almost all pop and electronic music, where a bar of 4/4 is your basic loop unit.

The counting system

Count beats out loud: "1 2 3 4" per bar in 4/4. Eighth notes split each beat in two, counted "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and" (written 1 & 2 &). This out loud counting is the single most effective rhythm tool in existence. Use it on every new piece for your first six months.

Counting practice in 4/4. Purple numbers show the count, say every number and "&", play on the ones under a note. Tap this on your knee before playing it.
Eighth note counting. The notes on "&" fall exactly halfway between foot taps.

Ties and dots

A tie (the curve connecting two notes on the same line or space) joins them into one longer note: play once, hold for the combined value. Here C is held for 4 + 2 = 6 beats across the bar line.

Upbeats

Some tunes start with a partial bar, called a pickup or upbeat (Happy Birthday starts this way, on beat 3 of a 3/4 bar). Count the missing beats silently, start playing where the notes begin.

6. Reading both hands together

On the grand staff, notes stacked vertically sound at the same moment. The reading skill is to scan vertically first (what sounds together on this beat), then horizontally (what comes next). Start with the most common beginner texture: melody in the right hand, long notes in the left.

Play these together: RH melody (top) with LH whole notes (bottom). Bar by bar: LH plays its note on beat 1 and holds, RH plays the melody. This is Au Clair de la Lune, your first fully notated two hand piece.

The hands together protocol (use for every piece, forever):

1. RH alone until comfortable. 2. LH alone until comfortable. 3. Hands together at half speed, counting out loud. 4. Only speed up when three clean repetitions in a row happen at the current speed.

Common tempo words, slowest to fastest: adagio (slow), andante (walking pace), moderato (moderate), allegretto (fairly quick), allegro (quick and lively). A metronome marking like ♩ = 80 means 80 quarter note beats per minute, which you can set exactly on the Tools metronome.

Remember volume comes from key speed. Forte is a faster arm drop, never a harder squeeze. Practicing a five finger walk at p, then mf, then f, with a relaxed arm throughout, is a technique exercise in itself.

8. The daily sight reading system

Sight reading, playing music you have never seen, is a separate trainable skill from learning pieces, and it is the skill that makes you fast at everything else. Three minutes daily from week 5 onward. The method:

Before playing (30 seconds), scan: time signature, key signature (Volume 3 explains these), lowest and highest note, and any repeated patterns. Find the trickiest looking bar and hear its rhythm in your head.

Set a pulse slower than you think you need. Count one empty bar out loud.

Play through without stopping. This is the golden rule. Wrong notes are fine, stopping is not. Stopping trains stopping. Keep your eyes on the page, not your hands, and try to look one note ahead of what you are playing.

Once only. Playing it again is practice, not sight reading. Tomorrow, new material.

Material: the drills below, then any beginner music you can find. The repertoire in Volume 5 stages A and B doubles as sight reading material once you are past it. Free graded sight reading exercises are also easy to find, search IMSLP for beginner piano books or use the reading exercises in each Volume 5 piece's practice notes.

9. Reading drills

Read each line cold, following the sight reading system. Come back on later days and transpose them to other five finger positions.

Drill 1: treble, steps and repeats around Treble G. RH in G position (thumb on G4).
Drill 2: treble, adds skips. RH in C position.
Drill 3: bass clef, steps and skips around Bass F. LH fingers 1 on G3, position covers C3 to G3.
Drill 4: bass clef, mixed rhythm with rests. Count out loud.
Drill 5: eighth notes and a dotted rhythm in the treble. Tap and count before playing.
