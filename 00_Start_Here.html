Scott's Piano Course

A complete beginner to confident pianist in 48 weeks. 30 minutes every morning. Built on real pianist training, no shortcuts.

The promise of this course. If you practice 30 focused minutes every morning and follow the weekly plan, in one year you will read music fluently, play all major and minor scales and arpeggios with correct fingering, play any chord in any key, perform real classical repertoire, and have the harmony skills to write chord progressions and basslines for electronic production. Every page of this course is aligned to that outcome.

Why this method works

This curriculum combines the sequencing used by the major piano method schools (Faber, Alfred, and the RCM and ABRSM grade syllabi) with what learning science has established about skill acquisition. The core principles, and where they show up in the course:

PrincipleWhat the research saysWhere you'll see it
Deliberate practice (Ericsson)Improvement comes from focused work on the edge of your ability with immediate feedback, not from repetition of what you can already do.Every session has a specific goal. You practice the hard two bars, not the whole piece from the top.
Spacing effect (Ebbinghaus 1885; Cepeda et al. 2006)Thirty minutes daily beats three hours once a week, one of the most replicated findings in learning science. Motor skills consolidate between sessions, largely during sleep (Walker et al. 2002).The whole plan is built around your daily 30 minute morning slot.
Interleaving (Rohrer & Taylor 2007)Mixing skill types in one session produces stronger long term retention than blocking one skill for weeks.Each session rotates technique, reading, and repertoire. You never do just one thing.
Chunking (Chase & Simon 1973)Experts perceive patterns, not individual units, and pattern recognition is trainable. Shown first in chess masters, since replicated in music reading.Reading is taught by interval and shape, chords are taught as hand shapes, scales as finger groups.
Slow practiceThe motor system encodes whatever you repeat. Slow and correct engrains correct. Fast and sloppy engrains sloppy. Standard conservatory doctrine, consistent with motor learning research on error-free acquisition.Metronome speed ladders. You earn tempo increases, never force them.
Retrieval practice (Roediger & Karpicke 2006)Testing yourself builds memory far better than re-reading or re-watching.Flashcards, milestone self tests every 4 weeks, and daily sight reading of unseen material.
Healthy technique first (Taubman school)Injury and plateaus come from tension, twisting, and finger-only playing. Arm weight and alignment come first.Volume 1 and 3 build the physical foundation before speed is ever requested.

What this course deliberately avoids: apps that light up falling notes (they train key-chasing, not reading), learning pieces only from YouTube hand videos (same problem), and skipping scales because they feel boring (they are the vocabulary of every piece and every chord progression you will ever play).

Rules of the road

1. Never practice pain. Tension, aching forearms, or wrist pain means stop, shake out, and check Volume 3's alignment section. Pain is a technique error signal, not a badge of effort.

2. Slow is fast. The tempo where you play correctly is your tempo. Speed is a byproduct of accuracy, never the goal of a session.

3. Small chunks, many reps. Two bars practiced ten times beats the whole piece played twice. Always know exactly what you are fixing.

4. Consistency beats intensity. A short session on a tired day keeps the chain alive. Missing two days in a row is the thing to avoid.

5. Count out loud. Feels silly, works better than anything else for rhythm. Non negotiable in the early weeks.

6. Finish with music. End every session playing something you enjoy, even if it is eight bars. It is what your brain remembers about practice.

Your instrument: Yamaha P-525 setup

Physical setup. Bench at a height where your forearm is level with the floor when your fingers rest on the keys, elbow hanging slightly in front of your torso. Sit on the front half of the bench, feet flat, centered on middle C. FC3A pedal under your right foot (pedal technique starts week 15, and your FC3A supports half-pedaling, which you will use for the Gymnopédie).

Settings. Voice: CFX Grand, plain, no layers or effects, so your ear learns one honest piano sound. Touch sensitivity: default (medium). Keep Virtual Resonance Modeling on, it teaches your ear how real strings ring together. Volume at a comfortable conversational level. Headphones are fine, but keep at least one session a week on the speakers so you hear an instrument in a room.

For the production track. The P-525 does USB and Bluetooth MIDI, so when Volume 4 section 9 arrives, you record chords, basslines, and arps straight into your DAW from the same keys you practice on.

The road to your U1

Your goal of an acoustic Yamaha U1 within a year is well matched to this course, and the P-525's wooden key GrandTouch-S action is Yamaha's Clavinova-grade action, so the transition will be gentle. Three things smooth it further. From phase 3 onward, switch the touch setting to "hard" for one technique session a week, an upright demands a touch more finger commitment and this builds it. From around month 6, find occasional time on real acoustics: dealer showrooms, church or community pianos, a teacher checkup visit, because no digital fully reproduces how an acoustic's sound blooms and decays. And when you shop, audition the exact U1 you would buy with the skills you will have by then: two octave scales soft then loud, a chord progression with pedal, and the opening of the Minuet. You will know within five minutes if the action and tone are right. Budget for tuning twice in the first year, uprights settle after a move.

Your first week, day by day

Full detail is in Volume 1 and Volume 6, but here is exactly how to start tomorrow morning:

Day30 minutes
Day 1Read Volume 1 sections 1 to 3 at the piano. Set your bench. Practice the posture reset and the arm weight drop exercise. Find every C and every D on the keyboard.
Day 2Posture reset. Name and press every white key A to G across the whole keyboard. Arm drops with finger 3, each hand. Learn finger numbers until instant.
Day 3C position both hands. Five finger walk ups and downs, hands separate, slow and even. First tune: Hot Cross Buns by finger numbers.
Day 4Five finger walks again, aim for even volume across all fingers. Mary Had a Little Lamb, right hand. Start Volume 2 section 1: the staff.
Day 5Five finger patterns. Mary Had a Little Lamb polished. Flashcards on the Tools page, treble landmarks, 3 minutes.
Day 6Everything so far, plus Ode to Joy right hand from Volume 1. Flashcards 3 minutes.
Day 7Review day. Play all three tunes. Week 1 checklist in Volume 6. Enjoy that you are officially a pianist.
A note on teachers. This course is designed to be self contained, but nothing catches physical habits like a human. If you can, book a single lesson with a local teacher around week 8 and again around week 24 purely as a technique checkup. Alternatively, video yourself playing once a month and check it against the posture and hand shape checklists in Volume 1. Your future wrists will thank you.
