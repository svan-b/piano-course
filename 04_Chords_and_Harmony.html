Volume 4: Chords and Harmony

How notes combine into chords, chords into progressions, and progressions into music. This is the volume that connects classical training to your production work.

One skill, every genre. The chords in a Bach chorale, a pop ballad, and a techno pad loop are built from the same materials taught here. By the end of this volume you can name, build, and play any major, minor, or seventh chord in any inversion, hear how progressions work, and play from a lead sheet, which is also exactly the knowledge you need to write harmony into a DAW.

1. Intervals, measured precisely

Volume 2 taught intervals by staff distance. Chords need one refinement: counting in half steps (semitones), because the exact size in half steps is what gives an interval its emotional color.

IntervalHalf stepsSound and famous example
Minor 3rd3Dark, melancholy. First two notes of Greensleeves.
Major 3rd4Bright, stable. "Oh when the Saints..."
Perfect 4th5Open. Here Comes the Bride.
Perfect 5th7Powerful, hollow. Star Wars theme opening leap.
Octave12Same note, higher. Somewhere Over the Rainbow.
The two thirds from C: C to E is 4 half steps (major 3rd, bright), C to E♭ is 3 half steps (minor 3rd, dark). Count half steps on the keyboard, black keys included: C, C♯, D, D♯, E is four steps up.

2. Triads: the basic chord

A triad stacks two thirds: root, 3rd, 5th. The bottom third decides everything:

Major triad = root + 4 + 3. C major: C E G. Bright, resolved.

Minor triad = root + 3 + 4. C minor: C E♭ G. Same root and 5th, only the middle note drops one half step, and the whole mood changes. This one half step is the most important half step in music.

C major (top) and C minor (bottom). Play with RH 1 3 5, LH 5 3 1. Alternate between them slowly and listen until the difference is obvious with your eyes closed.

Learning method (retrieval, not rereading): use the chord builder on the Tools page. Name a chord, build it in your head, play it, then check. Four chords a day, cycling through all of them across phase 2 and 3. Play every chord you build with both hands and listen for major versus minor.

Playing chords well: form the shape in the air above the keys first, then one relaxed arm drop lands all three notes exactly together, weight evenly spread. If notes splatter (not together), the drop was tense or the shape wasn't ready. Slow down, shape first, then drop. Release to resting weight the instant the sound starts.

3. Inversions: the same chord, rearranged

Any triad can be stacked with a different note on the bottom. Same three notes, same chord name, different arrangement and different color:

C major in root position (C on bottom), first inversion (E on bottom), second inversion (G on bottom), and back to root an octave up. Fingering RH: root 1 3 5, first inversion 1 2 5, second inversion 1 3 5.
First inversion on the keyboard: E G C. Practice the classic inversion drill: root position, first, second, root up the octave, then back down. Hands separate, then together. One key per day.

Why inversions matter enormously: smooth music moves between chords with the least possible hand movement, called voice leading. C to F in root position leaps the whole hand. C (C E G) to F in second inversion (C F A) moves two fingers by one step and keeps the thumb still. Every good chord progression on piano, and every good pad progression in a DAW, is built from inversions chosen to minimize motion.

Voice leading in action: C, F in second inversion, G in first inversion, C. No jump bigger than a step in any finger. Play it and feel how the hand barely moves.

4. Chords inside a key: Roman numerals

Build a triad on each note of a major scale using only that scale's notes, and you get the key's seven chords, numbered with Roman numerals. Uppercase means major, lowercase minor:

The chords of C major. I (C), ii (Dm), iii (Em), IV (F), V (G), vi (Am), vii° (B diminished, the odd one: two minor thirds stacked, tense, rare in beginner music). The same pattern holds in every major key: I IV V are major, ii iii vi are minor.

The three primary chords, I, IV, and V, harmonize thousands of songs by themselves. Add vi and you can play most of the pop and electronic canon. Roman numerals are how musicians think about progressions independent of key: "one, five, six, four" names the same musical move whether you play it in C or in F♯.

ProgressionIn C majorWhere you have heard it

Cadences, the punctuation of music: V to I is a full stop (authentic cadence), the tension-and-release you feel at the end of nearly everything. IV to I is the softer "Amen" ending (plagal cadence). Play C, F, G, then stop. Feel your ear demanding C. That demand is what harmony is.

Daily progression practice (phase 2 onward, 3 minutes): pick one progression and one key. Play it blocked, root position first, then with inversions for smooth voice leading, LH playing the root note, RH playing the chord. Loop it until it sounds like music, not homework. Change key tomorrow.

5. Accompaniment patterns: what the left hand actually plays

Real pieces rarely hold plain blocked chords. These four patterns cover most of what you will meet, in classical repertoire and in your own writing:

Pattern 1, blocked: the chord on beat 1, held. Hymns, ballads, pads.
Pattern 2, broken chord: root, 3rd, 5th, 3rd in a gentle loop. Folk songs and lullabies.
Pattern 3, Alberti bass: root, 5th, 3rd, 5th. The engine of Mozart and Clementi, you will meet it in the Sonatina in Volume 5.
Pattern 4, root-fifth-octave: the open, powerful shape under rock, cinematic, and electronic music. As a bassline it is genre-defining.

Drill each pattern on one chord until automatic, then run it through a whole progression. Combining pattern 4 in the left hand with block chords in the right is instant film score.

6. Seventh chords and color chords

Add one more third on top of a triad and you get a seventh chord, four notes, richer color. The three essential types plus two suspensions:

Major 7 (Cmaj7)major triad + major 3rdC E G BDreamy, floating. Neo soul, lo-fi, chillout.

The ii V I progression with sevenths: Dm7, G7, Cmaj7. Play it and you will recognize the sound of jazz immediately. Notice the voice leading: each chord shares notes with the next.

7. The circle of fifths

Arrange the 12 keys so each step clockwise adds a sharp (moves up a 5th), and everything in this volume becomes one picture:

Outer ring: major keys. Inner ring: relative minors (same key signature). Neighbors on the circle share 6 of 7 notes, which is why neighboring chords sound good together: the I, IV, V of any key are the key itself and its two neighbors. Dominant 7ths resolve one step counterclockwise. Reading assignments, transposing, writing progressions: it is all navigation on this wheel.

Circle drills: say the majors clockwise from C until fluent (C G D A E B F♯, then flats: D♭ A♭ E♭ B♭ F, back to C). Play roots around the circle with the LH. Later, play I IV V I in each key moving clockwise one key per day. By phase 4 this is your key-mastery engine.

8. Lead sheets: melody plus chord symbols

A lead sheet gives you the melody on one staff and chord symbols above it, and trusts you to build the accompaniment. It is how pop, jazz, and session musicians work, and the fastest way to a large repertoire.

Chord symbol crash course: a letter alone is major (C). "m" is minor (Am). Numbers add sevenths (G7, Cmaj7, Dm7). "sus" replaces the third (Gsus4). A slash gives the bass note: C/E means C major with E in the bass, i.e. first inversion.

Level 1 realization: RH plays the melody, LH plays the chord root as a whole note at each symbol.
Level 2: LH plays full triads, using inversions for smooth movement.
Level 3: LH plays an accompaniment pattern from section 5, matching the song's mood.

A practice lead sheet loop (I vi IV V in C). Blue symbols above are the chords. Play at level 1, then 2, then 3 across three sessions. Then transpose the whole thing to G. This exact loop, with pattern 4 in the left hand, is a complete song skeleton.

9. Harmony for electronic production

Everything above transfers directly into your DAW. This section is the bridge.

Minor is the home key of electronic music. Most house, techno, trance, and ambient sits in natural minor (the aeolian mode). Your A minor and E minor scale work is directly writing-relevant. The dorian mode (natural minor with a raised 6th, i.e. D to D on white keys) is the second sound to know: brighter minor, very common in deep house and Daft Punk style harmony.

Pad voicings: close root position triads sound small and boxy on a synth. Producers spread them: root and 5th low, full triad above, an octave doubled on top. Play the A minor pad voicing below and hear it fill the room. Rule of thumb: wide intervals at the bottom, close intervals at the top, and keep thirds out of the deep bass.

Basslines are built from the root, 5th, octave, and minor 7th of the current chord, in rhythm. Your T9 octave float exercise and pattern 4 are literally bassline practice.

Arpeggiators just play the chord you hold, in a pattern, in time. Because you now know inversions, you know why holding C E G versus E G C gives a different arp contour. Program arps by choosing the inversion whose contour you want.

Writing loops: a standard progression loop is 4 or 8 bars, one chord per bar. Recipe: pick a minor key, choose i VI III VII or i iv VI VII (in A minor: Am F C G or Am Dm F G), voice with smooth inversions in the RH while LH plays roots, then record it as MIDI and let your sound design do the rest. The keyboard skills making this effortless are exactly the phase 2 and 3 drills.

A minor spread pad voicing: A2 and E3 low (root and 5th), then A3 C4 E4 above. Left hand takes the bottom two, right hand the triad. Try it sustained with pedal.
MIDI keyboard bonus: because you are learning on weighted 88 keys, a MIDI controller will feel trivially easy, and everything here (voicings, inversions, basslines, arps) is played into the DAW rather than mouse-drawn, which is faster and sounds more human. Your velocity control from arm weight training translates into natural dynamics in recorded MIDI.

10. Training the ear

Two minutes inside your daily chord work, no extra time slot needed:

Sing what you play. Play a note, sing it, play the next scale note, sing it. Singing (badly is fine, nobody is listening) forges the ear-hand link faster than anything.

Quality game: eyes closed, play a random triad from the table in section 2, decide major or minor by ear, open eyes and check. Ten per week.

Interval anchors: use the famous-song table in section 1 to name intervals you hear in daily life. The bass drop, the doorbell, the train horn: everything is an interval.

Progression spotting: when a song is playing, try to hear whether the bass goes to the IV, V, or vi. You will be wrong for a month and then suddenly right forever.
