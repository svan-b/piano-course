Volume 1: Foundations

The body, the keyboard, and your first music. Everything else in this course stands on what you build here.

Why this volume matters. Pianists who plateau after two years almost always trace it back to habits formed in week one: collapsed hand shape, tense shoulders, staring at their hands. Spending your first month on these fundamentals is the fastest route to playing well, not a delay before the real learning starts. This is the real learning.

1. How your instrument works

On an acoustic piano, pressing a key throws a felt hammer at a string. The speed of the hammer, which comes entirely from how fast the key moves, decides how loud the note is. Your 88 key weighted digital piano simulates exactly this, which is why key weight matters: you are training the same muscles and the same control an acoustic demands.

Two consequences shape everything about technique. First, volume comes from key speed, so a heavy tense press sounds no louder than a fast relaxed drop. Second, once the note sounds, pressing harder does nothing. Tension after the sound starts is pure waste, and releasing it is one of the core skills you will practice.

The piano has 88 keys spanning just over 7 octaves. You will spend your first months in the middle, around the C nearest the maker's logo, called middle C.

2. Sitting at the piano

The setup, in order:

Bench distance. Sit on the front half of the bench. When you place your hands on the keys, your elbows should hang slightly in front of your torso, not pinned at your sides and not reaching. If your elbows are behind your body, move the bench back.

Bench height. With fingers resting on the keys, your forearm should be level with the floor, or a hair above. Wrist neither drooping below the keys nor arched above your knuckles. Add a firm cushion if your bench doesn't adjust.

Feet. Flat on the floor, right foot slightly forward near the sustain pedal. Your feet and sit bones carry your weight so your arms don't have to.

Back and shoulders. Tall but not rigid, like a string gently pulls the crown of your head up. Shoulders down and heavy. Check them every few minutes at first, they will creep up.

Position on the keyboard. Center yourself on middle C.

The posture reset. Do this at the start of every session and any time you feel tension: raise your shoulders to your ears, hold two seconds, let them drop completely. Let your arms dangle at your sides, totally limp, and feel their weight. That heavy, released arm is the arm you play with.

3. The hand

Let your arm hang limp at your side and look at your hand. It curves naturally, fingers gently rounded, as if holding a small orange. That exact shape, unmodified, is your playing shape. You never need to make a hand position, you only need to not destroy the natural one.

Contact point. You play on the pad of the fingertip, not the flat of the finger and not the nail. Keep nails short enough that you can play on slightly curved fingers without clicking.

The knuckle bridge. The row of knuckles across the top of your hand is a bridge that carries the arm's weight. It stays up. If a knuckle dimples inward when you play, the bridge has collapsed, lighten up and rebuild the shape.

The thumb. The thumb plays on the side of its tip, near the nail's corner. It is a short finger, so the hand does not twist to accommodate it, it just touches where it lands.

The wrist. Level and springy, never locked. Think of it as a suspension system between arm and fingers.

The one habit to police ruthlessly: flat, splayed fingers with a collapsed bridge. It feels easier for the first two weeks and it caps your speed and control forever. Rounded and relaxed, always.

4. Finger numbers

Every piece of piano music uses the same numbering. Thumbs are 1, pinkies are 5, on both hands. The hands mirror each other.

Thumb is always 1, pinky is always 5. Blue circled numbers appear above notes in sheet music to suggest which finger to use.

Drill until instant: away from the piano, call out random numbers and wiggle that finger on each hand. Then at the piano, rest both hands on any keys and press the finger you name. You want zero thinking time by the end of week 1.

5. Keyboard geography

The keyboard looks like 88 identical choices. It is actually one 12 note pattern repeating over 7 times. Learn the pattern once and you know the whole instrument.

The black keys come in alternating groups of two and three. Those groups are your landmarks.

Two octaves. Notice the repeating groups of 2 and 3 black keys. The white key names repeat A through G forever.

Finding C: C is the white key immediately to the left of every group of 2 black keys. Find every C on your piano right now, bottom to top. Say "C" out loud as you play each one.

Finding F: F is the white key immediately to the left of every group of 3 black keys.

D lives between the 2 black keys. It is the easiest key to find by touch alone, which is why we drill it early: eyes closed, grab any group of 2 black keys with fingers 2 and 3, and your finger between them is on D.

Every C, highlighted. Middle C is the C nearest the center of your piano, written C4 (the fourth C from the bottom).

Octaves. The distance from any key to the next key with the same name is an octave. Play middle C, then the next C up, then the next. Same note, higher register. Scientists write the octave number after the letter, so middle C is C4, the C above is C5. You will see this notation throughout the course.

Geography drills for week 1 and 2 (2 minutes a day): play and name every C, then every D, then every F and G. Then random call outs: "find all the E's." Then eyes closed: find D's and C's by feeling the black key groups. When you can put a finger on any named white key within a second, geography is done.

6. Your first sounds: the arm weight drop

Before any tune, you learn how sound gets made, because this single motion is the seed of all piano technique. The goal: a full, warm tone with zero effort.

The drop, step by step:

1. Do the posture reset. Let your right arm dangle limp at your side and feel its weight.

2. Lift the whole arm from the shoulder, hand relaxed and dangling, about 10 cm above the keyboard.

3. Let the arm fall onto middle C, landing on the rounded tip of finger 3. Do not press. Gravity plays the note, your finger just catches the arm like a leg catches your body weight in a step.

4. Let the wrist give slightly on landing, springy, then rest there with the key held down, arm completely relaxed.

5. Listen. A good drop sounds full and round. A press sounds thin and tight. Your ear learns the difference fast.

Do 10 drops with each hand on finger 3, on different keys around the middle of the piano. Then try fingers 2 and 4. Fingers 1 and 5 land on their own contact points (side of thumb tip, outside of pinky tip). This exercise opens every practice session in phase 1.

The release matters as much as the landing. After each drop, while holding the key, check: shoulder heavy? Wrist springy? Forearm loose enough that someone could wobble it? Holding a key down requires almost no force. Learn what "resting on the key" feels like.

7. C position and the five finger walk

C position is home base for your first month. Right hand: thumb on middle C (C4), one finger per key up to G. Left hand: pinky on the C below (C3), one finger per key up to G.

Right hand C position: fingers 1 to 5 on C4 D4 E4 F4 G4.
Left hand C position: fingers 5 to 1 on C3 D3 E3 F3 G3. Note the mirror: pinky on C, thumb on G.

Exercise 1, the walk (RH): play C D E F G, one note per slow beat, saying finger numbers aloud: "1 2 3 4 5." Then walk back down "5 4 3 2 1." Each finger drops with a little arm weight, the previous finger releases as the next lands, so notes connect smoothly. This connected style is called legato.

Exercise 2, the walk (LH): same keys one octave lower: "5 4 3 2 1" going up, "1 2 3 4 5" coming down.

Exercise 3, evenness: the hard part is not the notes, it is making finger 4 sound as strong as finger 3. Play the walk and listen for weak notes. Where a note is quiet, slow down and give that finger a touch more arm weight. Never force from the finger alone.

Exercise 4, skips: C E G E C ("1 3 5 3 1"), then C E C E G G ("1 3 1 3 5 5"). Skipping fingers builds independence.

Exercise 5, hands together (end of week 2): both hands walk up and down at the same time. Both hands play C together, then D together, and so on. Go embarrassingly slowly. Hands together is a brain skill, not a hand skill, and slow tempo is how the brain wires it.

Watch for the creeping thumb: thumbs like to slide off their key or hover in the air. Each finger rests lightly on its own key, ready, touching the surface.

8. Your first tunes

You will play these by finger numbers now, and again from real notation in Volume 2, so the same music teaches you twice. RH in C position unless marked.

Hot Cross Buns

RH, one number per beat, dash means hold for 2 beats3  2  1–   3  2  1–   1 1 1 1  2 2 2 2  3  2  1–

That is E D C, E D C, then repeated C's and D's, then E D C. Say the numbers as you play. When it flows, say the letter names instead.

Mary Had a Little Lamb

RH in C position. Finger numbers above, letters and counting below. This is real notation, Volume 2 teaches you to read it, for now match the numbers to the shape of the melody.

Ode to Joy (Beethoven, 9th Symphony theme)

RH in C position. The fourth bar has a dotted quarter note (1.5 beats) followed by an eighth note (half a beat), count it "1, 2 and, 3, 4" with the short note on "and". Just copy the rhythm from the audio in your head, everyone knows this tune.

When the right hand is comfortable, add the left hand: hold C3 with LH 5 for the first two bars, G3 with LH 1 for the third, back to C3 for the fourth. Long held bass notes under a melody is your first taste of real two handed playing.

9. Moving beyond one position

In week 3 and 4, start freeing yourself from home base:

Transposing: play the five finger walk and Hot Cross Buns starting on G instead of C (G A B C D). Same finger numbers, same shape, new sound. This is the seed of playing in every key, which Volume 3 develops fully.

Octave jumps: play C E G with RH 1 3 5, then lift the whole relaxed arm and land the same shape one octave higher. Arm moves, hand shape stays. Aim, float, land.

Middle C position: both thumbs share middle C (RH 1 on C4, LH 1 on B3). Many early reading pieces use this position, you will meet it in Volume 2.

Volume 1 exit checklist

You are done with this volume (typically end of week 4) when you can honestly check every box:

☐ I can set my bench and posture without thinking, and I catch my shoulders when they rise.
☐ My hand keeps its rounded shape and the knuckle bridge stays up while playing.
☐ Finger number call outs are instant, both hands.
☐ I can find any named white key within a second, and find C and D with my eyes closed.
☐ My arm weight drop sounds full and relaxed on any finger.
☐ Five finger walks are smooth and even, hands separately and slowly together.
☐ I can play all three tunes from memory, and Ode to Joy with the held bass notes.
