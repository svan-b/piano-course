Volume 5: Repertoire

Real music, in four graded stages, from your first tunes to Bach and Satie. Plus the system for learning any piece efficiently.

Repertoire is where everything integrates. Technique, reading, and harmony are trained separately so they can be combined here. Each stage below is calibrated to the phase you are in, and each piece is chosen to teach something specific. Play music you love, always have one piece that excites you on the stand, and remember: a piece is not finished when you can get through it, it is finished when you can play it while thinking about the music instead of the notes.

How to learn a piece: the system

Use this process for every piece from now until forever. It is the difference between three weeks and three months on the same music.

1. Survey (before playing a note). Look at the whole score: key signature, time signature, where sections repeat (most music is heavily repetitive, find the repeats and your workload halves). Listen to a recording twice, following along in the score.

2. Divide. Split the piece into sections of 4 to 8 bars. You will learn sections, never "the piece". A section a day is faster than the whole piece every day.

3. Hands separate, then together. RH alone slowly until easy, LH alone until easy (give the LH extra love, it is always the bottleneck), then hands together at half speed, counting aloud. The Volume 2 protocol.

4. The spotlight rule. Most of your time goes to the hardest two bars, not the comfortable opening. Practice trouble spots with the bar before and after, so the join is trained, not just the spot.

5. Ladder to tempo. Metronome, three clean reps per notch, exactly like scales. Performance tempo is the last thing to arrive, on its own schedule.

6. Never always start at the top. Practice starting from each section boundary. Pieces learned only from bar 1 collapse at the first slip, pieces with many entry points are robust.

7. Polish. When notes are secure: dynamics, phrasing (shape each line like a sentence with a rise and fall), pedal, and finally play-throughs where stopping is banned and musicality is the only goal.

Memory comes free. By the time a piece survives this process you will mostly know it by heart. Test it: play a section eyes closed. Memorized repertoire is what you can play when someone says "play something!", keep two pieces performance ready at all times from phase 2 onward.

Stage A First music, weeks 1 to 10

Five finger positions, simple rhythms, hands mostly separate or with held bass notes. Hot Cross Buns, Mary Had a Little Lamb, and Ode to Joy live in Volume 1, Au Clair de la Lune in Volume 2. Three more here:

Frère Jacques, and your first duet with yourself

RH in C position, except finger 5 stretches to A briefly in bar 5 and 6 (fingering shown). Teaches: eighth notes in a real tune, and the phrase "G8 A8 G8 F8" is your first four note run.

The canon trick: this tune is a round. Once solid, play it in RH while the LH plays a held C3-G3 open fifth. Then, for a real challenge in week 9 or 10: start the LH playing the tune (one octave down) two bars after the RH. Your first counterpoint, the texture Bach is made of.

Jingle Bells (chorus)

RH, C position. Teaches: repeated notes with a loose wrist (let the hand bounce lightly, one impulse per note, no grabbing), and the dotted quarter rhythm on "way".

When the Saints Go Marching In

RH, C position, simplified traditional arrangement. Starts after a quarter rest, count "1" silently. Teaches: rests inside phrases, and long held notes with counting. When solid, add LH: C3 on the first beat of every bar, switching to G3 for bars 8 and 11 (listen and let your ear place it, this is chord-sense training).

Stage B Both hands as partners, weeks 11 to 24

Melody plus real accompaniment, the pedal arrives, first minor key music, first lead sheet playing.

Amazing Grace

First half, in C, 3/4 time. Begins on beat 3 (count "1, 2" then play). Chord symbols above for the LH: play level 1 (roots), then level 2 (triads on beat 1), then pattern 2 from Volume 4 (broken chords). Teaches: 3/4 time, pickup upbeats, lead sheet realization, legato pedal (change on each chord).

House of the Rising Sun (arpeggio study)

The LH pattern for the first two chords, Am and C. Continue the same broken shape through the full progression, one bar per chord: Am, C, D, F, Am, E, Am, E, then repeat. Add the pedal, one change per chord. Teaches: LH arpeggiated accompaniment, minor key harmony, and it sounds gorgeous far beyond its difficulty, this is a piece to play for people.

Stage C Real classical repertoire, weeks 25 to 38

Minuet in G major (Petzold, from the Anna Magdalena Bach Notebook)

RH melody, first 8 bars. The most famous student piece ever written, and deservedly: it teaches elegant fingerwork, 3/4 dance lilt (slight emphasis on beat 1), and phrases that rise and fall like breathing. Fingering: bar 1 starts 5, the runs walk naturally under the hand.

Practice notes: get the full score free at IMSLP (Minuets BWV Anh. 114-115). Learn the LH from the score, it is simple quarter note lines and its independence from the RH is the real lesson. Section by section, hands separate first, always. Target tempo around ♩ = 104, but it is charming at 80. This is your week 36 recital piece.

Gymnopédie No. 1 (Satie)

The two LH shapes that carry the whole piece: bass G then a Gmaj7 shell (B D F♯), alternating with bass D then Dmaj7 (A C♯ F♯). One per bar, slow 3/4, deep pedal.

Practice notes: free score at IMSLP (3 Gymnopédies). The texture is exactly Volume 4's "bass note plus chord" with maj7 colors, which is why producers adore this piece, it is ambient music from 1888. LH alone until the bass-chord swing is automatic and calm, then float the famous melody on top with the RH. Teaches: pedal artistry, voicing (melody louder than accompaniment), patience, and taste. Slow pieces hide nothing, this will be the best tone workout of your year.

Stage D The destination pieces, weeks 39 to 48 and beyond

Bach, Prelude in C major (WTC Book 1, BWV 846)

Bar 1 pattern, simplified rhythm. The whole prelude is this one ripple applied to a changing chord each bar: LH holds the two bottom notes, RH ripples the top three, everything repeats twice per bar. First four chords: C, Dm7/C, G7/B, back to C.

Practice notes: score at IMSLP (Well-Tempered Clavier I). This piece is a 35 bar chord progression study disguised as a masterpiece, the perfect marriage of your classical and production tracks. Learn it as chords first: block each bar's five notes as one chord, play the whole piece as blocked chords (it takes a day and teaches you the harmony), then unroll the ripple. Steady, calm, no rushing, let the harmony do the emoting.

Chopin, Prelude in E minor (Op. 28 No. 4)

Practice notes: score at IMSLP (Preludes Op. 28). RH plays a nearly static lament, LH plays repeated chords that sink by half steps, pure voice leading, the Volume 4 skill at its most expressive. Block the LH chords and learn the progression first (it reads like a modern chord chart). Teaches: repeated chords with weight control (each softer than melodramatic), rubato, and restraint. Real Chopin, genuinely within reach at week 44 or so.

Clementi, Sonatina Op. 36 No. 1, first movement

Practice notes: score at IMSLP (6 Sonatinas Op. 36). The classical style in miniature: C major scale runs (your Volume 3 work, verbatim), Alberti bass (Volume 4 pattern 3, verbatim), crisp articulation. If the Minuet was your elegance exam, this is your velocity exam. Sparkling at ♩ = 120, learnable at 90. A year one graduation piece.

Aspirational shelf (year two openers)

Für Elise (Beethoven, the A section is easier than its fame suggests), Satie's Gnossienne No. 1, Bach's Minuet in G minor (the Anna Magdalena companion piece), Burgmüller's Ballade, and the Moonlight Sonata first movement, which is arpeggio playing and voicing, both of which you will own by then. All free on IMSLP.

Sourcing scores

IMSLP is the great free library of public domain scores, every classical piece above is there. On your iPad, download the PDF and read it in Books or any score reader app side by side with these volumes. Mutopia offers cleanly re-typeset free editions of many standards. For folk tunes, any simple arrangement works, or trust your ear, they were never written down definitively anyway.

Performing, even for an audience of zero

Record a mini recital at weeks 12, 24, 36, and 48: phone on the music rest, play your two best pieces, one take, no stopping. The recording does three jobs: it simulates performance pressure (you will be amazed how the red dot affects your hands, and how that fades with repetition), it documents progress (week 12 you will cringe at, week 36 you will send to people), and it audits technique, watch your wrists and shoulders like a teacher would.

Performance rules: slower than practice tempo, always. Never stop for mistakes, the listener forgets a wrong note in one second but remembers a stop forever. Bow ironically if you must, but finish every piece.

Volume 5 exit standard

By week 48: the Minuet in G, Gymnopédie No. 1, and the Bach Prelude performed confidently on a recording, two pieces memorized and always ready, one Burgmüller étude polished, the Clementi underway, and at least three original loops of your own in your DAW. That portfolio is a genuinely accomplished first year, on pace with a strong traditionally-taught student.
